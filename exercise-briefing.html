The premise

Where the meter fits in the scenario

Each blue team runs a facility, and the smart meter on their kit is that facility's power. So this little box of lights is not a toy on your desk. It is the power feeding a facility you are responsible for understanding.

When the scenario attack fires, that power faults, the operators lose their normal way to recover, and the question lands in your lap: what happened, and what should they have done about it?

What this brief answers

  • How a radio message becomes a physical consequence.
  • What makes an update channel trustworthy, or not.
  • How an auditor would even see this coming.

The point is understanding: the same meter sits in your blue team's environment, and this is how it can be turned against them.

Grounding it - the real world

How a real smart meter talks

To your home
Zigbee - a short-range 2.4 GHz mesh reaching in-home displays and smart thermostats.
To the utility
A license-free ~900 MHz radio mesh, hopping meter to meter up to a neighborhood collector, then back to the utility.
For updates
The utility pushes firmware and configuration over that same radio to millions of meters - the channel that also carries the reading and, on many meters, a remote disconnect.

Why that is a soft target

A meter is not just a sensor. It can physically connect or disconnect a home. The radio that manages it reaches enormous scale, and historically the crypto that protects it arrived late and unevenly. An attacker who can speak on that channel is one message away from a physical effect, repeated across a whole neighborhood.

A little OT theory

IT protects data. OT moves the physical world.

What "OT" means

Operational Technology is the computing that runs physical processes: meters, valves, motors, breakers. When OT is attacked, the failure is not a leaked file. It is voltage, water, or power doing the wrong thing.

Why the rules are different

OT devices live for decades, rarely reboot, and were built for reliability, not authentication. Many field protocols simply trust anyone who can reach them: no login, no signature. That was safe when the wire was locked in a substation. Radio breaks that assumption.

So the meter is the perfect teaching target: a real controller, driving a physical output you can watch, speaking the same trusting protocols the real grid runs on.

The kit · component 2 of 4

The four-light panel - an HMI

02/04

An HMI, a Human-Machine Interface, is how a person sees and commands a physical process. In a control room it is a wall of screens. On our bench it is deliberately stripped to its essence: four indicator lamps. If a light is on, that state is true.

What the lights mean

  • Blue and green - operator-set, "I'm here and configured."
  • Yellow - voltage is up, the dial is past its threshold.
  • Red - fault. The meter is down.

The whole drama lives here: when the attack lands, blue/green/yellow drop and red comes on, even though the operator's switches are still set. The panel at the top of this deck does the same thing on the attack slides. That gap between "what the operator asked for" and "what the meter is doing" is the attack, made visible.

The kit · component 4 of 4

The LoRa radio - the update channel

04/04

LoRa ("Long Range") is a low-power radio built to send small messages a long way, kilometers, on license-free spectrum. It is the same family of technology behind real utility and smart-city networks. No SIM, no subscription: turn it on and talk.

Its job in the kit

A LoRa module sits on each Pi, and every meter listens on the same frequency. When the attacker transmits, every radio in range hears it at once. That broadcast nature is exactly what lets one message reach a whole room of meters.

Why this radio: it is cheap, legal to transmit on, and simple to inject into from a second node, so the attack can be practiced safely while keeping the properties that matter.

The language the meter speaks

Modbus - the lingua franca of OT

Modbus is a 45-year-old industrial protocol still running everywhere. Its model is dead simple: a device exposes numbered registers and coils you can read or write. No accounts, no permissions. If you can reach it, you can command it.

That simplicity is why it is everywhere, and why "reachable" and "authorized" being the same thing is a problem the moment the network is not physically locked down.

WhatAddressMeaning
POWER_STATUScoil 0healthy (1) / faulted (0)
VOLTAGEreg 0live volts
POWERreg 1watts drawn
FW_MODEreg 90 normal · 1+ faulted
RESETcoil 15operator clear

The one register that matters: FW_MODE. Normally 0. Push it off 0 and the meter faults. The entire attack is a single write to that number.

A modeling choice worth understanding

The "malicious firmware" is a mode change

We do not actually reflash the meter. That would be slow, fragile, and impossible to reset dozens of times. Instead, the malicious update flips the FW_MODE register, and the meter's own logic reacts.

It is a model, but a faithful one. The teaching point was never "code got rewritten." It was "a message on the update channel changed what the device physically does." That is exactly what happens.

Why model it this way

  • Re-runnable - resets in seconds, all day long.
  • Faithful - a write into OT still changes the physical output.
  • Two doors - because the meter owns the logic, both the radio and a direct network write trigger the same fault.

The payload still looks like firmware - magic bytes, a version, a checksum - so the exercise can teach integrity and supply-chain, not just "a bit got flipped."

The attack surface

The channel checks the message, not the messenger

The update frame is shaped like real firmware: a magic tag, a version, a type, and a checksum the gateway validates. So the gateway "knows" the message is well-formed.

But a checksum only proves the bytes were not corrupted, not that they came from someone trusted. There is no signature and no identity on this channel. Anyone can build a perfectly valid update.

the attack, in one line
# craft a valid update and transmit it
$ ./listener.py --send malicious
# every meter in range faults

The lesson, in one sentence

"It passed validation" is not "it is trustworthy." Integrity checks catch accidents. Only authentication catches an adversary, and this channel has none, by design.

Kept benign-but-present: normal traffic carries ordinary version heartbeats too, so the lesson is "the channel is unauthenticated," not "any radio traffic is bad."

Why one shot hits many

One transmission floods the whole mesh

Because LoRa is a broadcast, every meter in earshot hears the update at once. But it goes further: each meter that receives it also rebroadcasts it once to its neighbors, so the update ripples outward hop by hop, reaching meters the attacker could not touch directly.

This is exactly how a real mesh spreads a legitimate update. The attacker just borrows the same mechanism.

Two simple rules keep it sane

  • A message ID means each meter acts once, no endless echo.
  • A hop limit bounds how far it travels.

Same code, any scale: a tight bench is a one-hop flood; a spread-out site is true multi-hop. Nothing new is needed, it is built on the radios already in every kit.

Why it matters

Trust the channel, and you hand the switch to anyone with a radio.

What to take away

  • Update and management channels are OT attack surface.
  • Integrity is not authenticity - a checksum is not a signature.
  • A logic change in a controller has a physical result.
  • One broadcast can become many victims.

Seeing it is the hard part

In theory a defender watches the air for a rogue update and the meter for an unexpected write. In practice that is a research project: the right radios, captured keys or deep protocol knowledge, a legal tap into the OT network, and a baseline to tell normal from malicious. Most OT shops have none of it. Knowing the attack exists, and being able to advise on it, is the realistic win.

Bottom line for the OPFOR: you do not need a zero-day. You need a valid checksum and the nerve to hit send. The grid has trusted every polite stranger with a radio for fifty years - so go be a polite stranger.
